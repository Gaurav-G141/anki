# Physics GRE (PGRE) Study Workflow — physicsgre.com Forum Research

**Method note:** physicsgre.com sits behind Cloudflare and returns HTTP 403 to direct
fetching, so this research was assembled entirely from WebSearch results — i.e. from
search-engine-indexed thread _titles_ and the short _snippet excerpts_ the engine
extracts from each thread. I have **not** read full thread bodies. Where a point comes
from a snippet I treat it as a paraphrase of something a forum poster actually wrote;
where a point is only implied by a thread _title_ I flag it as inferred. Snippet
paraphrases can blur multiple posters together, so treat specific numbers as
"reported on the forum" rather than verbatim quotes.

The forum's structure itself is a signal: the relevant board is **"Registration and
Test Preparation"** (`viewforum.php?f=18`) and **"Problems, Solutions, and Discussion"**
(`viewforum.php?f=19`), with several long-lived _sticky_ threads that the community
treats as canonical (Preparation Overview, 4 Practice Tests & Solutions, Books &
Materials).

---

## Summary

The dominant, repeatedly-stated PGRE study workflow on physicsgre.com is a
**practice-test-driven diagnostic loop**, not a textbook-first review:

1. The exam is highly standardized and recycles concepts, so the **4 released ETS
   practice tests (GR8677, GR9277, GR9677, GR0177)** are the center of gravity for
   nearly all prep.
2. Take a practice test **early as a diagnostic**, score it, and use it to find weak
   topics — explicitly "think of them as diagnostic tests rather than practice tests."
3. **Review every missed/guessed problem**, typically against **grephysics.net**
   (community worked solutions) and a textbook for the underlying concept.
4. Do **focused, weak-area-driven** textbook review (Halliday-Resnick-Walker is the
   most-cited anchor; _Conquering the Physics GRE_ is the most-cited condensed review),
   not comprehensive re-reading.
5. **Repeat** the loop with the remaining tests, and **save the last test for ~1 week
   before** the real exam under fully timed, bubble-sheet conditions to gauge readiness
   and practice **speed/timing**.
6. **Speed is the binding constraint** (~1.7 min/problem). A huge share of the advice is
   about pacing, when to skip, when to guess, and how to bubble without errors.

Recurring pain points: running **out of fresh material**, **timing/finishing**, deciding
**how much to memorize vs. derive**, **guessing strategy**, and **bubbling mistakes**.
Recurring wants: a structured weekly schedule, more practice problems, fast formula
recall, and a way to retain material across a long prep window. Study groups
(in-person, IM/phone historically, **Discord** more recently) appear repeatedly and are
endorsed as helpful-but-optional.

## Themed findings (with sources)

### 1. Typical prep timelines

- A month and a half is reported as "more than sufficient" to prepare adequately, though
  needs vary by person. — _Advice on How to Study For the Physics GRE_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5210
- For someone working full-time, **~3 months** is described as an acceptable window to
  reach a realistic score; one concrete weekend-only plan: ~24 study days × ~5 hrs ≈
  **~120 hours** total. — _Doing gre physics without physics or engineering background_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=165187 ; reinforced in _Physics GRE
  Preparation Advice - Overview_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- Recommended cadence elsewhere: **1–3 hours/day**, most of it on practice problems. —
  _Physics GRE Preparation Advice - Overview_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- Test is offered only **April, September, October**; most majors sit it Sept/Oct of
  senior year, and getting the _general_ GRE done early (junior year) is advised to free
  up time for the physics test. — snippet context from
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5210 and https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=105
  (_GRE timeline_)
- Taking it **early / a year early** via self-study is feasible because only a few
  questions come from upper-level coursework; an April junior-year attempt doubles as a
  diagnostic with summer to re-study before a fall retake. — _Taking PGRE a Year Early?_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=4069 ; _Mentioning Self-Study_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5821
- Threads exist specifically for people quitting work to study full-time and for
  non-physics backgrounds, signalling a range of timelines/intensities. — _Leaving job
  and preparing for the GRE physics in full time_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=182205 (timeline inferred from title);
  _Doing gre physics without physics or engineering background_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=165187
- A behavioral tip: **register and pay the fee early** to use loss-aversion against
  procrastination. — https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=165187

### 2. The core study routine / workflow

- Canonical loop (from the sticky Overview): review fundamental principles & equations →
  work sample problems → review them → study related material → take a sample test →
  review it → study related material → **repeat**. Begin by reviewing lower-division
  coursework fundamentals. — _Physics GRE Preparation Advice - Overview_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- "The best and most basic way to study is to look at previous papers" because the test
  is standardized and the real paper won't differ much. — https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- Practice-tests-first as **diagnostics**: do all available practice questions before
  "hardcore reviewing," treat tests as diagnostics, then attack weak areas; for familiar
  topics fully understand the solution, for unfamiliar ones ask professors / use
  textbooks / Wikipedia. — _Textbook revision vs practice GRE exams_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1867
- "The secret of success is having good knowledge in **all** topics, not just mastering
  the ones you already know well" — i.e. don't over-index on strengths. — _How to
  practice for physics gre when out of study material?_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830

### 3. How they use the 4 released practice exams

- Sticky thread aggregates the **4 practice tests + solutions** as the central resource.
  — _==> Sticky: Physics GRE 4 Practice Tests and Solutions_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2548
- Suggested spacing: **one test early**, **one before the midway point**, and **save the
  last for a few weeks before the exam** to work specifically on speed and timing. —
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5210 ; "take a practice exam under test-like
  conditions about once a month" — https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- **Reserve one test for ~1 week before** the real exam under fully realistic timed
  conditions (including a real bubble sheet) and use that score as your readiness
  indicator. — _Mentioning Self-Study_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5821
- Don't try to "learn the material" _from_ the tests — use them to surface what you're
  missing; ideally don't burn the newest test as your first diagnostic. — _How to
  practice ... out of study material?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830
- Each test's per-problem Q&A is discussed thread-by-thread, so the community functions
  as a crowd-sourced solution set. — _Questions/Answers GR0177 - GR9677 - GR9277 -
  GR8677_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=109

### 4. How they review mistakes

- After doing the tests, list every problem you **couldn't do or had to guess**, then
  pull the worked solution (community consensus points to **grephysics.net**) and study
  the underlying concept. — _The key to success on Physics GRE..._,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1782 ; _How to practice ... out of study
  material?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830
- **Redo missed problems until you both understand them and can solve them in <3 min**;
  if a problem still takes too long, get help from someone strong at PGRE physics. —
  _How can I break 600 on this test?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3515
- Use first-year textbooks to patch specific gaps a condensed prep book skipped (named
  examples in-snippet: lenses, thermodynamic cycles). —
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830
- "Doing many problems" is valued because it builds deeper conceptual understanding and
  lets you discover your own **shortcuts** for PGRE-style problems. —
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830

### 5. Speed / timing / pacing

- The framing constant: **~1.7 minutes per problem** (100 questions / 170 min) — invoked
  as the reason you cannot derive everything from first principles. — _Memorizing
  Formulas_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3957
- Concrete pacing heuristics (reported): aim for ~**17–19 questions per 30 minutes**,
  check the clock after ~7–8 questions to confirm you're on pace, and **never spend more
  than ~5 minutes** on a single question. — _ne last minute tips for pgre?_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1780
- Multi-pass strategy: do the easy ones first, then the "pretty sure but slow" ones, then
  return to the hard ones — so you bank guaranteed points before time pressure. —
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1780
- **Skip complicated questions initially** rather than getting stuck. — same thread,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1780
- Improvement stories attribute big jumps mainly to fixing **time management** (e.g.
  reported 840 → 900 → 980 across April/Sep/Oct). — _How to practice ... out of study
  material?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830 ; _Re-Take Physics Gre? How
  to improve guessing?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5721

### 6. Guessing strategy (and the scoring/penalty question)

- Whether wrong answers are penalized is a recurring confusion: older posts / a 2014 book
  cite a **1/4-point penalty**, but the 2017 ETS booklet says **nothing is subtracted**
  — so under current rules, **always guess**. — _Are PGRE Incorrect Answers Penalized or
  Not?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=127254
- Expected-value math for elimination: eliminating **2** wrong choices ≈ ~80% chance of
  net-positive (only ~8% net-negative); eliminating only **1** ≈ ~54% positive / ~24%
  negative. — _Probability for getting points from guessed answers_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=4835 ; _Guessing and Average PGRE Scores_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2220
- Practical split: confidently answer ~70–80, then **guess intelligently** on the
  remaining 20–30. — https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2220
- A whole thread is devoted to _improving guessing as a skill_. — _Re-Take Physics Gre?
  How to improve guessing?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5721

### 7. Bubbling / answer-sheet mechanics (a distinct, underappreciated workflow concern)

- Real lost points from **misbubbling** — typically skipping a question but forgetting to
  skip the row. — _Filling in the Bubbles_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2231
- Mitigations: keep one finger on the test problem number and one on the bubble number;
  prefer to **circle answers in the booklet and bubble in a sweep** near the end rather
  than after each question; reserve a few minutes to **cross-check bubbles** at the end. —
  same thread, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2231

### 8. Formula memorization vs. derivation

- Dedicated thread; reported consensus: the test "is about memorizing formulas," but
  there aren't that many — **memorize the basic ones**, and for harder/complex formulas
  just know their general behavior. — _Memorizing Formulas_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3957
- Because of the ~1.7 min/problem limit, deriving from (e.g.) Maxwell's equations is "not
  suggested"; rule of thumb across physics forums — **memorize what you can't quickly
  derive**, and let frequently-used equations get memorized naturally through doing many
  problems. — same thread, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3957
- "Key to success" sticky: memorize/understand the equations & definitions in HRW's
  shaded boxes and chapter summaries; ~10% of the test is "random facts," the rest tracks
  Halliday-Resnick-Walker. — _The key to success on Physics GRE..._,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1782
- **Flashcards** are recommended in combination with practice tests (a poster cites a
  phys.cwru.edu flashcard set as "an excellent study combination"); formula lists circulate,
  including a ~14-page list. — _Memorizing Formulas_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3957
- Note: I found **no** physicsgre.com thread explicitly naming **Anki / spaced
  repetition** (the targeted search surfaced only generic Anki pages and an Art-of-Memory
  "memory palace for PGRE formulas" thread off-site). So spaced-repetition tooling appears
  to be a _gap_ in the forum's stated toolkit rather than an established practice. —
  inferred from absence; cf. off-forum
  https://forum.artofmemory.com/t/constructing-my-first-memory-palace-learning-all-the-physics-formulas-for-the-physics-gre/28524

### 9. Materials / books they actually use

- **Halliday-Resnick-Walker (Fundamentals of Physics)** = the most-cited anchor text. —
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1782
- _**Conquering the Physics GRE**_ = the most-cited condensed review; praised as a
  time-saver (review ~10 pages/topic vs. full textbooks); one reported jump 720 → 990
  using it. — _New Physics GRE prep book available_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=4771 ; _Are These Books Enough?_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=181926
- Other named resources: _3000 Solved Problems in Physics_ (Halpern); subject texts
  (Taylor CM, Griffiths E&M and QM, Tipler/Llewellyn Modern); GRE Physics by REA ("purple
  book"); Sterling Test Prep practice questions; ETS publications. — _The key to
  success..._, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1782 ; _==> Sticky: Books and Other
  Preparation Materials_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=2559 ; _Book review:
  Sterling Test Prep..._, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=6353
- Community-generated practice supplements exist because official material is finite:
  "User Created 70 Sample Questions" and ~15 community-submitted problems. — _Physics GRE
  Practice Problems_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1073 ; forum index
  https://physicsgre.com/
- A known frustration: there is **no single comprehensive Princeton-Review-style book**,
  so students must stitch together many sources. — _New Physics GRE prep book available_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=4771

### 10. Running out of material (a real workflow wall)

- An entire thread is "what do I do once I've exhausted the practice tests / prep book?"
  Answers: take a **~1-month break to flush memory** of problems then redo tests; use
  intro textbooks for gap topics; use advanced HS multiple-choice problems for
  speed/accuracy/endurance. — _How to practice for physics gre when out of study
  material?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173830 ; _Working Practice Problems
  on the Physics GRE_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1063
- The "redo after a memory-clearing break" idea recurs as the standard way to reuse a
  finite test bank. — https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3515

### 11. Study groups & social workflow

- Studying with peers is reported as "very beneficial and enjoyable," but explicitly
  _optional_ — some prefer solo. — _Physics GRE Preparation Advice - Overview_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1057
- The forum is actively used to **organize** groups by test date and by geography
  (in-person Caltech-library group meeting Saturdays, with remote participation via
  IM/phone/email historically; later a **Discord** server for a Sept cohort). —
  _Online Study Group_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=4136 ; _Study group online
  for April 2014_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5229 ; _gre physics group
  september 2017_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=127215 ; _PGRE 2019
  April/Sep/Oct/Nov Study group_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=173779

### 12. Goals, scores, retaking (motivational context shaping the workflow)

- Score-goal setting is data-driven on the forum: raw→scaled conversions (~46–59 raw ≈
  700–790), per-school acceptance "green/blue/red" zones, and target percentiles (theory
  ~70s–80s+; aim 900+ for top theory). — _Setting Score Goals_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5123 ; _Acceptance by PGRE Score Summary_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=3669 ; _The 700 Club_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1040
- Retake only with a concrete reason to improve (real new studying, or first score was a
  fluke); "just tossing the dice again" isn't worth it. Documented improvement is
  possible, especially from fixing timing. — _Retaking the Physics GRE_,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1783 ; _Re-Take Physics Gre?..._,
  https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=5721

### 13. Test-day / last-minute behavior

- Night before: skim notes, run a broad topic overview; the morning is for refreshing,
  not learning. Anxiety-prone people are told **not** to cram or pull all-nighters. —
  _ne last minute tips for pgre?_, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1780
- Mindset: no single question determines the score; relax to avoid fatigue/panic. — same
  thread, https://physicsgre.com/viewtopic.php?t=1780

## User wants / needs (implications for a study app)

These are inferred from the patterns above; the strength of evidence is noted.

1. **A guided, schedule-driven practice-test loop.** Strongly evidenced. The community
   re-derives the same workflow (diagnostic test → review misses → targeted study →
   repeat → save last test for timed dress-rehearsal) over and over. An app that _builds
   the schedule for them_ around their test date and the 4 tests — and tells them which
   test to take when — directly automates the most-repeated advice.
   (Sources: t=1057, t=1867, t=5210, t=5821, t=2548.)

2. **Mistake-driven, per-problem review tied to solutions and the underlying concept.**
   Strongly evidenced. Users manually list missed/guessed problems and look them up on
   grephysics.net. An app should auto-log misses, link a worked solution, tag the
   _topic_, and surface "you keep missing X." (Sources: t=173830, t=1782, t=3515.)

3. **Topic-level weakness tracking and "cover all topics" balancing.** Strongly evidenced.
   The explicit warning is not to over-study strengths. A per-topic mastery dashboard
   (CM, E&M, QM, Thermo/Stat, Optics/Waves, Relativity, Atomic, Lab, Specialized) maps
   onto how they already think. (Sources: t=1867, t=173830; topic mix from the
   "Questions/Answers" board and t=109.)

4. **Timing/pacing trainer.** Strongly evidenced and emotionally charged. Given
   ~1.7 min/problem, per-problem timers, pace alerts ("you're behind"), and analytics on
   _where_ time is lost would target the factor most credited for score jumps.
   (Sources: t=3957, t=1780, t=173830.)

5. **A built-in guessing/EV coach.** Evidenced. Confusion over the penalty and elimination
   EV is widespread; the app can simply encode "no penalty → always answer," and nudge
   guessing once N choices are eliminated. (Sources: t=127254, t=4835, t=2220.)

6. **Formula/fact recall with spaced repetition — likely an unmet need.** Medium evidence
   _for the need_, strong evidence the _current tooling is ad hoc_ (loose flashcard sets,
   14-page formula PDFs, off-site "memory palace" experiments) and **no forum mention of
   Anki/SR**. A curated PGRE formula deck with spaced repetition directly fills a gap they
   feel ("the test is about memorizing formulas") without a good tool. (Sources: t=3957,
   t=1782; gap inferred from absence + off-forum Art-of-Memory thread.)

7. **A renewable / larger problem bank.** Strongly evidenced. "Out of study material" is a
   named wall; their fixes (1-month memory-clearing break, scrounging HS problems) are
   workarounds. Generated/extra PGRE-style problems, or smart re-surfacing of old ones
   after a delay, would be valued. (Sources: t=173830, t=1063, t=1073, t=4771.)

8. **Realistic exam simulation, including bubble mechanics.** Evidenced and specific.
   Full-length, fully-timed mode that mimics the answer-sheet workflow (and catches
   skip-a-row errors) addresses a concrete, recurring source of lost points. (Sources:
   t=5821, t=2231.)

9. **Cohort / study-group features.** Medium evidence. They repeatedly self-organize by
   test date and have migrated to Discord; date-based cohorts, shared progress, or group
   challenges fit existing behavior — but it's explicitly optional, so make it opt-in.
   (Sources: t=4136, t=5229, t=127215, t=173779, t=1057.)

10. **Goal-setting + readiness signal.** Medium evidence. They translate raw→scaled→
    percentile→school-tier manually. Letting a user set a target score/school and showing
    a predicted readiness band from practice performance matches how they reason.
    (Sources: t=5123, t=3669, t=1040.)

11. **Accountability / anti-procrastination and anxiety-aware pacing.** Lighter evidence.
    "Pay the fee to commit," "don't cram the night before," reminders/streaks and a sane
    taper before test day align with stated behavior. (Sources: t=165187, t=1780.)
