## Problem 1

**Topic:** Mechanics → Acceleration Vector
**Answer:** C

A pendulum bob oscillates; identify the diagram correctly showing the acceleration vector of the bob at various points of its swing.

**Solution:** The acceleration has two components: a tangential component (maximum at the endpoints where $v=0$, zero at the bottom) and a centripetal/normal component directed toward the pivot ($a_c = v^2/L$, zero at endpoints, maximum at the bottom where $v$is maximum). Applying Newton's second law, the net force is the vector sum of string tension and gravity $mg$. At the endpoints the net acceleration points back toward the center (tangential); at the bottom the centripetal acceleration points up toward the pivot. Because gravity always contributes, the acceleration is never purely radial except at the lowest point. The diagram consistent with these directions is choice (C).

## Problem 2

**Topic:** Mechanics → Centripetal Force
**Answer:** D

A coin rests on a rotating turntable spinning at $33\tfrac{1}{3}$rev/min; the coefficient of friction is $\mu \approx 0.3$. Find the maximum radius at which the coin stays put.

**Solution:** Friction supplies the centripetal force: $\mu m g = \frac{m v^2}{r}$, so $r = \frac{v^2}{\mu g}$. Convert the angular speed: $\omega = 33.3 \text{ rev/min} = 33.3 \cdot \frac{2\pi}{60} \approx \pi \text{ rad/s}$, giving $v = \omega r \approx \pi r$. Substituting: $r = \frac{(\pi r)^2}{\mu g} = \frac{\pi^2 r^2}{\mu g}$. Dividing by $r$: $1 = \frac{\pi^2 r}{\mu g}$, so
$$r = \frac{\mu g}{\pi^2} \approx \frac{0.3 \cdot 10}{10} \approx 0.3 \text{ m}$$using $\pi^2 \approx g \approx 10$. The answer is (D).

## Problem 3

**Topic:** Mechanics → Kepler's Third Law
**Answer:** D

Determine how the orbital period $T$of a satellite depends on the orbital radius $R$.

**Solution:** Kepler's Third Law states that the square of the period is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius: $T^2 \propto R^3$, hence $T \propto R^{3/2}$. Derivation: gravity provides centripetal force, $\frac{GMm}{R^2} = \frac{mv^2}{R}$, so $v \propto R^{-1/2}$. The period is $T = \frac{2\pi R}{v} \propto \frac{R}{R^{-1/2}} = R^{3/2}$. The answer is (D).

## Problem 4

**Topic:** Mechanics → Conservation of Momentum
**Answer:** C

An object of mass $2m$moving with velocity $v$collides with and sticks to a stationary object of mass $m$. What fraction of the kinetic energy is lost?

**Solution:** Conservation of momentum: $2mv = 3mv'$, so $v' = \tfrac{2}{3}v$. Initial KE: $\frac{1}{2}(2m)v^2 = mv^2$. Final KE: $\frac{1}{2}(3m)v'^2 = \frac{3}{2}m\left(\frac{2}{3}v\right)^2 = \frac{2}{3}mv^2$. Fraction of KE lost:
$$1 - \frac{KE_f}{KE_i} = 1 - \frac{2/3 \, mv^2}{mv^2} = \frac{1}{3}$$The answer is (C). (Shortcut for a stationary target: fractional loss $= \frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2} = \frac{m}{3m} = \frac13$.)

## Problem 5

**Topic:** Thermodynamics → Equipartition Theorem
**Answer:** (value = $3kT$; answer letter not shown on page)

Find the average total energy of a three-dimensional harmonic oscillator in thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$.

**Solution:** By the equipartition theorem, each quadratic degree of freedom in the Hamiltonian contributes $\frac{1}{2}kT$to the average energy. A 3D harmonic oscillator has 3 quadratic kinetic terms ($\frac{p_x^2}{2m}, \frac{p_y^2}{2m}, \frac{p_z^2}{2m}$) and 3 quadratic potential terms ($\frac{1}{2}kx^2$, etc.), for 6 quadratic terms total. Thus
$$\langle E \rangle = 6 \cdot \frac{1}{2}kT = 3kT$$

## Problem 6

**Topic:** Thermodynamics → Work in Different Processes
**Answer:** E

A gas expands from $(P_1, V_1)$to final volume $V_2 = 2V_1$by two routes: an isothermal process and an adiabatic process. Compare the work done, $W_a$(adiabatic) vs $W_i$(isothermal).

**Solution:** Isothermal work: $W_i = nRT \ln\frac{V_2}{V_1} = P_1 V_1 \ln 2 > 0$. Adiabatic work: $W_a = \frac{1}{1-\gamma}\left(1 - 2^{1-\gamma}\right)P_1 V_1 > 0$. On a $P$–$V$diagram, starting from the same point, the isothermal curve ($P \propto 1/V$) lies above the adiabatic curve ($P \propto 1/V^\gamma$), since the adiabat falls off faster. Because work equals the area under the curve, the isothermal process does more work, while the adiabatic still does positive work. Therefore $0 < W_a < W_i$, answer (E).

## Problem 8

**Topic:** Electromagnetism → Conductors
**Answer:** D

A point charge $+Q$is placed outside a grounded conducting plane. What is the total charge induced on the plane?

**Solution:** A grounded conductor induces the opposite charge to a nearby external charge. Electrons in the (grounded) conductor are drawn toward the positive charge, and because the plane is grounded, the displaced positive charge is whisked away to ground, leaving a net induced charge of $-Q$. By the method of images, the grounded plane is equivalent to an image charge $-Q$, confirming the total induced surface charge is $-Q$. The answer is (D).

## Problem 9

**Topic:** Electromagnetism → Electric Field
**Answer:** A

Five equal point charges are placed at the vertices of a regular pentagon. Find the electric field at the center.

**Solution:** By symmetry the field at the center is zero. Each charge produces a field of magnitude $\frac{kq}{r^2}$pointing radially, but the five vectors are separated by $\frac{2\pi}{5} = 72^\circ$and sum to zero. Formally, rotating the configuration by $72^\circ$maps it onto itself, so the field must be invariant under that rotation; the only vector invariant under such a rotation is the zero vector. Hence $\vec{E} = 0$, answer (A).

## Problem 10

**Topic:** Electromagnetism → Capacitors
**Answer:** A

Two capacitors $C_1 = 3\,\mu\text{F}$and $C_2 = 6\,\mu\text{F}$are connected in series across $V = 300$V. Find the total energy stored.

**Solution:** Series equivalent capacitance:
$$C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2} = \frac{3 \times 6}{3 + 6} = 2\,\mu\text{F}$$Energy stored:
$$U = \frac{1}{2}C_{eq}V^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2\times10^{-6})(300)^2 = \frac{1}{2}(2\times10^{-6})(9\times10^4) = 9\times10^{-2}\text{ J} = 0.09\text{ J}$$The answer is (A). (Note: series/parallel formulas for capacitors are opposite to those for resistors.)

## Problem 11

**Topic:** Optics → Thin Lens Formula
**Answer:** A

Two converging lenses are separated by 30 cm. An object is placed 40 cm in front of the first lens (focal length 20 cm); the second lens has focal length 10 cm. Find the final image position.

**Solution:** Thin lens equation: $\frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f}$. First lens: $\frac{1}{40} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{20} \Rightarrow d_i = 40$cm (image 40 cm right of lens 1). Since the lenses are 30 cm apart, this image lies 10 cm beyond the second lens, acting as a virtual object: $d_o = -10$cm. Second lens: $\frac{1}{-10} + \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{10} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{10} + \frac{1}{10} = \frac{1}{5}$, so $d_i = 5$cm to the right of the second lens. The answer is (A).

## Problem 12

**Topic:** Optics → Mirror Formula
**Answer:** (correct choice corresponds to a virtual image; letter not clearly shown on page)

An object is placed inside the focal length of a concave mirror ($d_o < f$); describe the resulting image.

**Solution:** Mirror equation: $\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}$. Solving, $\frac{1}{d_i} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{d_o}$. When $d_o < f$, the term $\frac{1}{d_o} > \frac{1}{f}$, making $\frac{1}{d_i} < 0$, so $d_i < 0$. A negative image distance indicates a virtual image located behind the mirror (and it is upright and magnified). The correct choice is the one describing a virtual image.

## Problem 14

**Topic:** Mechanics → Cross Section / Solid Angle
**Answer:** C

A gamma-ray detector of diameter $d = 8$cm registers 50% of emitted rays when placed adjacent to the source. What fraction is detected when moved $R = 1$m away?

**Solution:** The fraction detected equals the ratio of detector area to the total spherical area over which the isotropic radiation spreads. Detector area $A = \frac{\pi d^2}{4}$; spherical area at distance $R$is $4\pi R^2$. Thus
$$\text{Fraction} = \frac{\pi d^2/4}{4\pi R^2} = \frac{d^2}{16 R^2} = \frac{(0.08)^2}{16(1)^2} = \frac{64\times10^{-4}}{16} = 4\times10^{-4}$$The answer is (C).

## Problem 16

**Topic:** Lab Methods → Counting / Poisson Statistics
**Answer:** (value = 5000 s; letter not shown on page)

A radiation counting experiment records on average 2 counts per second. How long must one count to reduce the fractional (statistical) uncertainty to 1%?

**Solution:** For Poisson-distributed counts, the standard deviation equals the square root of the total number of counts: $\sigma = \sqrt{N_{\text{total}}}$. With a rate of 2 counts/s over time $t$(seconds), $N_{\text{total}} = 2t$. The fractional uncertainty is
$$\frac{\sigma}{N_{\text{total}}} = \frac{\sqrt{2t}}{2t} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2t}} = 0.01$$Solving: $\sqrt{2t} = 100 \Rightarrow 2t = 10^4 \Rightarrow t = 5000$s.

## Problem 17

**Topic:** Atomic Physics → Electron Orbital Configuration
**Answer:** B

Identify the correct ground-state electron configuration (for phosphorus).

**Solution:** Ground-state electrons fill the lowest-energy orbitals first (aufbau principle), following the order 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, .... Choices that promote electrons to higher orbitals before filling lower ones, or that skip the 3s subshell entirely, are eliminated. Phosphorus (Z = 15) does not have enough electrons to reach the 4s or 3d states, and transition (d-block) elements only begin filling 3d after 4s. The configuration that correctly follows the diagonal filling rule is choice (B): $1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6\,3s^2\,3p^3$.

## Problem 18

**Topic:** Atomic Physics → Bohr Theory
**Answer:** A

The total energy to remove both electrons from a helium atom is 79.0 eV. Find the ionization energy of the first electron.

**Solution:** After the first electron is removed, He⁺ is a hydrogen-like ion with $Z = 2$. Its (second-electron) ionization energy follows the Bohr scaling $E = Z^2 \cdot 13.6\text{ eV}$:
$$E_2 = (2)^2 (13.6) = 54.4 \text{ eV}$$The total equals the sum of both ionization energies:
$$79.0 = E_1 + 54.4 \Rightarrow E_1 = 79.0 - 54.4 = 24.6 \text{ eV}$$The first electron is easier to remove (less bound) due to electron-electron repulsion. The answer is (A), $\approx 24.6$eV.

## Problem 19

**Topic:** Astrophysics → Stellar Nucleosynthesis
**Answer:** B

Concerning the Sun's primary thermonuclear (proton-proton) reactions producing helium, identify the correct accounting of the reaction products/particles.

**Solution:** It takes 4 hydrogen nuclei (protons) to build one helium-4 nucleus in the Sun's proton-proton chain. The overall reaction is
$$4\,^1\text{H} \;\rightarrow\; {}^4\text{He} + 2e^+ + 2\nu_e + \text{photons}$$Helium-4 contains 2 protons and 2 neutrons; converting 4 protons into this requires turning 2 protons into neutrons via $p \rightarrow n + e^+ + \nu_e$(i.e., 1 positron and 1 neutrino emitted per neutron formed). Accounting for all of helium's constituents and the emitted leptons gives the correct choice (B).

## Problem 21

**Topic:** Atomic Physics → Bohr Model / Spectral Line Transitions
**Answer:** B

A hydrogen atom emits radiation in the Lyman series and the Balmer series. The problem asks for the ratio of the wavelength of a Lyman line to that of a Balmer line.

**Solution:** Using the Rydberg/Bohr formula, the photon energy (and inverse wavelength) is proportional to $\left(\frac{1}{n_f^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2}\right)$.

For the Lyman line ($n_i=2 \to n_f=1$): $1 - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{4}$.

For the Balmer line ($n_i=3 \to n_f=2$): $\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9} = \frac{5}{36}$.

Energy ratio: $\frac{E_L}{E_B} = \frac{3/4}{5/36} = \frac{27}{5}$.

Since $\lambda \propto 1/E$, the wavelength ratio is the reciprocal: $\frac{\lambda_L}{\lambda_B} = \frac{5}{27}$. Answer: (B).

## Problem 22

**Topic:** Mechanics → Orbital Mechanics
**Answer:** A

A moon orbits a planet in an elliptical orbit. Given the minimum distance $r_{min}$and maximum distance $r_{max}$from the planet, along with the orbital speed at one point, the question asks which quantity CANNOT be determined.

(A) The mass of the moon
(B) The velocity at apoapsis
(C) The velocity at periapsis
(D) The orbital period
(E) The semi-major axis

**Solution:** The semi-major axis follows from geometry, $2a = r_{min} + r_{max}$, eliminating (E). Conservation of angular momentum at the two apsides, $m v_{min} r_{max} = m v_{max} r_{min}$, lets you find the other velocity — the moon's mass $m$cancels, eliminating (B) and (C). Energy conservation, $\frac{1}{2}m v_{max}^2 - \frac{GmM}{r_{min}} = \frac{1}{2}m v_{min}^2 - \frac{GmM}{r_{max}}$, again has $m$cancel and lets you solve for the planet's mass $M$. With $a$and $M$known, Kepler's third law $\tau^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{GM}$gives the period, eliminating (D). The moon's mass cancels out of every gravitational equation, so it can never be recovered. Answer: (A).

## Problem 23

**Topic:** Mechanics → Vectors / Circular Motion
**Answer:** (corresponds to 45°)

A particle moves in a circle of radius $r = 10$m. At the instant its speed is $v = 10$m/s, its speed is increasing at a tangential rate of $a_t = 10$m/s². Find the angle between the total acceleration vector and the velocity vector.

**Solution:** The acceleration has two perpendicular components. Tangential: $a_t = 10$m/s². Centripetal (normal): $a_n = \frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{(10)^2}{10} = 10$m/s². Since the two components are equal in magnitude and mutually perpendicular, the resultant makes an angle $\theta = \arctan\!\left(\frac{a_n}{a_t}\right) = \arctan(1) = 45^\circ$with the tangential (velocity) direction.

## Problem 25

**Topic:** Mechanics → Moment of Inertia
**Answer:** E

Seven identical pennies (uniform disks of mass $m$, radius $r$) are arranged with one penny at the center and six pennies surrounding it (each touching the center penny). Find the moment of inertia of the configuration about an axis through the center of the central penny, perpendicular to the plane.

**Solution:** For a disk about its own center, $I = \frac{1}{2}mr^2$. Central penny: $I_{center} = \frac{1}{2}mr^2$. Each outer penny's center is a distance $d = 2r$from the system axis. By the parallel-axis theorem:
$$I_{outer} = \frac{1}{2}mr^2 + m(2r)^2 = \frac{1}{2}mr^2 + 4mr^2 = \frac{9}{2}mr^2.$$Six outer pennies: $6 \times \frac{9}{2}mr^2 = \frac{54}{2}mr^2$. Total:
$$I = \frac{1}{2}mr^2 + \frac{54}{2}mr^2 = \frac{55}{2}mr^2.$$Answer: (E).

## Problem 26

**Topic:** Mechanics → Conservation of Energy (Rotation)
**Answer:** C

A uniform rod of length $L$and mass $M$stands vertically with its lower end fixed to the ground as a pivot. It is released from rest and falls, rotating about the fixed end. Find the speed of the free (upper) end when it strikes the ground.

**Solution:** The center of mass falls a height $L/2$. Using energy conservation about the pivot with $I_{pivot} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2$:
$$Mg\frac{L}{2} = \frac{1}{2}I_{pivot}\,\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}ML^2\,\omega^2.$$Solving: $g = \frac{1}{3}L\omega^2 \Rightarrow \omega = \sqrt{\frac{3g}{L}}$. The free end speed is $v = \omega L = \sqrt{3gL}$. Answer: (C).

## Problem 27

**Topic:** Quantum Mechanics → Hermitian Operators
**Answer:** (statement that eigenvalues are real)

The problem asks which statement about a Hermitian operator is true (concerning the nature of its eigenvalues).

**Solution:** For a Hermitian operator $\hat{A}$with $\hat{A}^\dagger = \hat{A}$and eigenvalue equation $\hat{A}\psi = a\psi$:
$$\langle\psi|\hat{A}|\psi\rangle = a\langle\psi|\psi\rangle, \qquad \langle\psi|\hat{A}^\dagger|\psi\rangle = a^*\langle\psi|\psi\rangle.$$Since $\hat{A}^\dagger = \hat{A}$, these are equal, giving $a = a^*$, so $a$is real. The eigenvalues of a Hermitian operator are always real (Hermitian operators represent observables, whose measured values must be real).

## Problem 29

**Topic:** Quantum Mechanics → Expectation Value
**Answer:** C

A system is in the normalized state $\psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\psi_{-1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\psi_{1} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\psi_{2}$, where the $\psi_n$are orthonormal eigenstates of an operator $\hat{O}$with eigenvalues $-1$, $1$, and $2$. Find the expectation value $\langle \hat{O}\rangle$.

$$\langle \hat{O}\rangle = \frac{1}{6}(-1) + \frac{1}{2}(1) + \frac{1}{3}(2) = -\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{2}{3} = 1.$$The expectation value is $1$, choice (C).

## Problem 30

**Topic:** Atomic Physics → Hydrogen Atom Wave Functions
**Answer:** A (the exponentially decaying radial function)

The problem presents candidate radial functions and asks which could represent a hydrogen-atom radial wave function. The candidates are roughly: (I) $Ae^{-br}$, (II) $A\sin(br)$, (III) $A/r$.

**Solution:** A physical (bound-state) radial wave function must be normalizable, $\int_0^\infty |R(r)|^2\, 4\pi r^2\, dr < \infty$. (II) $A\sin(br)$oscillates forever and is not normalizable over all space — rejected. (III) $A/r$gives $\int_0^\infty (A/r)^2\,4\pi r^2\,dr = 4\pi A^2\int_0^\infty dr$, which diverges — rejected. (I) $Ae^{-br}$decays exponentially, vanishes at infinity, and is square-integrable. Hydrogen radial functions are exponentially decaying; only the angular (spherical harmonic) parts involve trig functions. Answer: (A).

## Problem 31

**Topic:** Atomic Physics → Energy Levels / Positronium
**Answer:** A

A positronium atom (a bound electron-positron pair) undergoes a transition between energy levels (e.g., $n_i = 3 \to n_f = 1$). The problem asks for the energy of the emitted photon.

## Problem 32

**Answer:** D

A particle of rest mass $m$has total energy equal to twice its rest-mass energy, $E = 2mc^2$. Find its momentum.

**Solution:** Use the relativistic energy-momentum relation:

## Problem 33

**Topic:** Special Relativity → Time Dilation / Reference Frames
**Answer:** D

A pion with proper (rest-frame) lifetime $\tau_0 = 3\times10^{-8}$s travels 30 m in the laboratory before decaying. Find its speed.

**Solution:** The 30 m is measured in the lab; the lifetime $\tau_0$is proper time, so the lab-frame lifetime is dilated, $t = \gamma\tau_0$. With $L = vt = v\gamma\tau_0$:
$$30 = \frac{v\,\tau_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}.$$Squaring: $900\left(1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}\right) = v^2\tau_0^2$, so
$$v = \sqrt{\frac{900}{1.01\times10^{-14}}} \approx 2.98\times10^{8}\text{ m/s} \approx 0.99c.$$Answer: (D).

## Problem 34

**Topic:** Special Relativity → Spacetime Interval / Simultaneity
**Answer:** C

Two events are separated in space by $\Delta x$and in time by $\Delta t$. The problem asks for the condition under which there exists a reference frame in which the two events are simultaneous.

**Solution:** Events can be simultaneous in some frame only if they are spacelike separated, i.e., the invariant interval satisfies
$$s^2 = c^2\Delta t^2 - \Delta x^2 < 0 \Rightarrow \Delta x^2 > c^2\Delta t^2 \Rightarrow \left|\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\right| > c.$$Equivalently $c\,\Delta t < \Delta x$: the events lie outside each other's light cones and cannot be causally connected. A suitable Lorentz boost then makes them simultaneous. Answer: (C).

## Problem 36

**Topic:** Thermodynamics → Adiabatic Processes
**Answer:** E

An ideal gas undergoes reversible adiabatic expansion. The problem asks which statement about the process is correct.

**Solution:** By definition of adiabatic, $dQ = 0$. For a reversible adiabatic process the entropy change is $dS = dQ_{rev}/T = 0$, eliminating choices that claim heat flow or entropy change. The first law gives $\Delta U = Q - W = -W$: since the gas does positive work expanding, its internal energy (and temperature) decreases. Adiabatic is not the same as isothermal — from $TV^{\gamma-1} = \text{constant}$, a volume change forces a temperature change. The correct statement is the one acknowledging this (the gas cools / it is not isothermal). Answer: (E).

## Problem 37

**Topic:** Thermodynamics → Cycle Analysis (Work)
**Answer:** D

A gas is taken around a closed cycle through points A, B, C on a $P$-$V$diagram: A→B is isobaric at $P = 200$kPa, B→C is isothermal, and C→A is isochoric. Point C is at $P_C = 500$kPa, $V_C = 2$L. Find the net work done over the cycle.

**Solution:** First find $V_B$from the isothermal segment, $P_B V_B = P_C V_C$with $P_B = 200$kPa:
$$V_B = \frac{P_C V_C}{P_B} = \frac{500 \times 2}{200} = 5\text{ L}.$$Work on each leg:

- C→A (isochoric): $W = 0$.
- A→B (isobaric): $W = P\,\Delta V = 200\text{ kPa}\times(5-2)\text{ L} = 600$kJ.
- B→C (isothermal): $W = P_C V_C \ln\!\frac{V_C}{V_B} = (500\times2)\ln\!\frac{2}{5} \approx -916$kJ.

Net: $W = 0 + 600 - 916 \approx -300$kJ (the cycle runs counterclockwise, giving negative net work). Answer: (D).

**Topic:** Electromagnetism → AC Circuits (Resonance)

## Problem 80

**Topic:** Special Relativity → Relativistic Velocity Addition (Fizeau experiment)
**Answer:** D

## Problem 88

**Topic:** Electromagnetism → Biot–Savart Law
**Answer:** C

A wire carries current $I$along straight radial segments connected by a circular arc of radius $R$subtending angle $\theta$at point P (the arc's center). Find the magnetic field at P.

**Solution:** Biot–Savart: $d\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi}\frac{d\vec{l}\times\hat{r}}{r^2}$. The straight segments have $d\vec{l}\parallel \hat{r}$, so their cross products vanish and they contribute nothing. For the arc, $d\vec{l}\perp\hat{r}$and $r=R$:

## Problem 89

A child of mass $m$stands at the rim of a uniform rotating disk (mass $M$, radius $R$) spinning at angular velocity $\omega$. The child walks to the center. Find the new angular velocity (numerically, $\approx 2.8$rad/s).

A round object of mass $M$and radius $R$rolls without slipping down from height $H$, reaching the bottom with speed satisfying $v^2 = \tfrac{8gH}{7}$. Find its moment of inertia.

## Problem 99

**Answer:** D

## Problem 100

**Topic:** Optics → Michelson Interferometer
**Answer:** B

In a Michelson interferometer, displacing the movable mirror produces 85,865 fringe shifts with red light ($\lambda_{red} = 632.82$nm) and 100,000 fringe shifts with green light over the same displacement. Find the green wavelength.

**Solution:** A fringe shift occurs each time the path changes by $\lambda$(mirror moves $\lambda/2$), so $m = 2d/\lambda$for fixed displacement $d$. Thus $m\lambda = 2d$is constant:
$$m_{red}\lambda_{red} = m_{green}\lambda_{green} \Rightarrow \lambda_{green} = \lambda_{red}\frac{m_{red}}{m_{green}}.$$$$\lambda_{green} = 632.82 \times \frac{85865}{100000} \approx 543\text{ nm} \approx 540\text{ nm}.$$This is choice (B).
