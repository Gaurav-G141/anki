## Problem 1

**Topic:** Mechanics â†’ Newtonian Mechanics
**Answer:** B

A stone is thrown vertically upward in the presence of air (wind) friction. The motion is governed by $m\ddot{x} = -k\dot{x} - mg$, where the drag force acts opposite to the velocity. The question asks which statement about the acceleration is true.

**Solution:** At the highest point of the trajectory the velocity is zero, $\dot{x}=0$, so the drag term vanishes. Substituting into the equation of motion:
$$\ddot{x} = \frac{1}{m}(-k\cdot 0 - mg) = -g.$$Thus at the apex the acceleration equals $-g$(gravity alone), since the velocity-dependent drag force is momentarily zero. Answer (B).

## Problem 2

**Topic:** Mechanics â†’ Central Forces / Orbital Motion
**Answer:** A

A satellite in a circular orbit around Earth briefly fires a control jet directed toward Earth's center, imparting a small radial velocity component. What shape does the resulting orbit become?

**Solution:** Gravity is the only force and is central, so angular momentum is conserved (no torque). The radial impulse adds a small inward velocity while leaving the tangential velocity essentially unchanged, slightly increasing the total energy. For an inverse-square force the orbit type depends on total energy: $E_t<0$gives a bound ellipse, $E_t=0$a parabola, $E_t>0$a hyperbola. Since the perturbation is small, the energy stays negative, so the orbit becomes an ellipse (with the original radius now serving as apogee or perigee). For circular orbits $v=\sqrt{GM/r}$and $L=m\sqrt{GMr}$; the unchanged $L$but altered $E$rules out a new circular orbit. Answer (A).

## Problem 5

**Topic:** Mechanics â†’ Conservation Laws (Energy & Momentum)
**Answer:** A

A piece of putty (mass $M$) falls from height $h_0$and collides perfectly inelastically with a stationary piece of putty of mass $3M$. To what height does the combined mass rise?

**Solution:**

1. Energy before collision: $Mgh_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_1^2$, giving the speed of $M$at impact.
2. Momentum during inelastic collision: $Mv_1 = (M+3M)v_2 = 4Mv_2 \Rightarrow v_2 = v_1/4$.
3. Energy after collision: $\tfrac{1}{2}(4M)v_2^2 = 4Mgh_3$. Substituting $v_2 = v_1/4$:
   $$\tfrac{1}{2}(4M)\frac{v_1^2}{16} = 4Mgh_3 \Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{2}M\frac{v_1^2}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{1} = 4Mgh_3.$$Using $Mgh_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}Mv_1^2$gives $h_3 = h_0/16$. Answer (A).

## Problem 12

**Answer:** E
